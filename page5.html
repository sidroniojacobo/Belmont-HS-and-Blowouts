A Brief Timeline of
Belmont High School

Since the 1930's, Belmont High School has served a diverse student body. While today it serves largely Mexican, Central American, and Filipinx students, throughout its history it has serve students from Cuba, Mexico, Nicaragua, South Korea, China, and other countries around the world. Below you will find a brief timeline of student enrollment from foreign countries. In addition, there are several visuals providing analysis and evidence.

1924

Belmont High School Opens its doors for students 

Located in central Los Angeles, Belmont HIgh School becomes the home school for students who live west of downtown Los Angeles, east of Hollywood, north of South Central LA, and south of North East LA (Craig, 1845; Crowther, 1967) 

Early 1940's

Foreign Adjustment Classes

The Los Angeles Unified School Districts archive offers interesting documentation of how Los Angeles came to serve the largest population of authorized and unauthorized immigrants during the 20th century. In particular, Belmont High School is a school that is highlighted in several documents relating to immigrant students as early as 1943. The Immigration Acts of 1921 and 1924 developed an immigrant quota system that generally favored South-Eastern European countries in efforts of maintaining a hegemonic racial order within U.S. society.

1945-1947

Belmont High School is approved by the U.S. Immigration Services to accept and enroll non U.S. Students

With the passage of the Immigration and Naturalization Act of 1965, immigration policies changed to be more focused on reuniting immigrant families and attracting skilled labor to the United States. During that in between period, Belmont High School became the third high school to accept students from countries that were listed as “non-quota” countries, such as Mexico, before 1965 (Crowther, 1945). There are ample telegraphed memos between the principal of Belmont (Benshimol, 1945), the office of the LAUSD Superintendent (Findlay, 1947), and the Commissioner for Immigration and Naturalization that discussed that accepting students, from countries listed as non-immigration quota, was a service to the United States as it served as reversed “peace corps” service.

1963

Belmont High school enrolls a larger number of Cuban students

By 1960’s, Belmont High School was receiving the most “foreign” students in LAUSD. For example, 140 Cuban students were in Belmont in 1963 (Crowther, 1963). It should not be surprising as the neighborhood, Echo Park, adjacent to Belmont High School had a strong enclave of Cubans. Today, Jose Marti’s statue overlooks the historic lake that is the core for the neighborhood.

1968

Realignment of Service Boundaries for HS programs for non-English and foreign students.

By 1967, the superintendent proposed “realignment of service boundaries” for secondary school programs for non-English and foreign students, as the population of immigrant students had rose by 59% since 1961, in which most students which came from Mexico (Crowther, 1967). By 1968, Belmont High School had the most number of nonimmigrant students at any senior high school in the LAUSD.                          

1970's

Belmont HS continues to enroll diverse number of students.

By the 1970’s, Belmont High School had been home to students who were Japanese, White, Black, Cuban, Nicaraguan, Mexican, Pilipino, Korean.
