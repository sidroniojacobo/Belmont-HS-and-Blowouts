Belmont High School & the 
 1968 Chicano Blowouts 

In 1968, thousands of high school students marched in the streets of Los Angeles demanding for better schooling. Today, these walkouts are known as the Chicano Blowouts of 1968. Over 50 years later, their legacies remain a reminder of the power of students and the possibilities that comes of collective power. By employing historical and data visualization methods, this project focuses on the efforts of students at one high school: Belmont High School.

About the Project
Introduction
The Chicano Blowouts refer to a series protests and marches which mobilized thousands of students who were seeking improved educational facilities, curriculum and administrator accountability (Berta-Avila, Tijerina-Revilla, & Figueroa 2011; Garcia & Castro, 2011; Munoz, 1989). The Chicano Blowouts were historic because they displayed the largest mobilization of Chicano youth leaders in the United States but also as the largest mobilization for school reform in American history. As the 50th year anniversary approaches, scholars interested in this phenomenon can benefit from case study analysis of how events unraveled at any of the five main high schools: Roosevelt High School, Garfield High School, Lincoln High School, Wilson High School, and Belmont High School. This project takes on this effort through a focus on Belmont High School, home of the Sentinels. Scholarly work on the Chicano Blowouts overlooks the unique contributions of BHS students and community organizers and the significant events than transpired there. This  project provides a historical case study of the involvement of students, families, and community partners in rallying support at Belmont High School, of downtown Los Angeles.
